On the left is a red fox cranium, and on the right are the hipbones from the same fox. Obviously, I'm not totally sure, but I think that this red fox was an older adult female. You can see that the hipbones are conjoined, like I physically can't separate them. This is rather unusual, and I haven't seen it in any of my other specimens. After some light research, I found out that this fusion happens slowly overtime, which leads me to believe that this is an older, experienced fox. I also have another red fox skeleton that is considerably larger than this one, and since this fox is clearly not an adolescent and female red foxes are on average smaller than male red foxes, I deduced that this red fox was probably an older lady. And I love her very much.




These are some teeth from a groundhog (I had just finished cleaning up the skeleton so that's why I'm wearing gloves). The long ones up top are the incisors. Groundhogs sometimes refered to as woodchucks but I think that sounds dumb are rodents, and a major component of being a part of the order Rodentia is having incisors that grow indefinitely. Rodents need to constantly use their incisors to grind them down, otherwise they can actually curl back up into their lips and harm them. These teeth slide back really far into the skull which allows for a lot of muscle connection to the jaw. That's why these rodents have really powerful bite forces, which helps them chew through tough vegetation. The other teeth here are molars, which are in the back of the mouth and used for grinding down plants even further. If you look closely, you might notice some brown and black discoloration. This is actually some tooth decay, which I find super interesting. Clearly, this groundhog was not brushing his teeth.






This is a raccoon baculum. And, um, so... hear me out... this is the penis bone. That's why I put it last, so only people who were actually interested in all of this would see it. So although it is kinda funny to think of the idea of a bone in the penis, they're actually really common in most mammals. They're even present in most primates, including our closest relative (the chimpanzee). So there is a lot of discussion in the scientific community on why humans don't have a baculum. As for the function of the baculum, it is obviously used in mating. It basically allows the male to mate for an extended period of time with a female because in the animal kingdom, females aren't always, let's say, amenable to mating. Pretty dark, I know. So their purpose in the animal kingdom is probably why humans eventually lost them! We enter long-term relationships with our partners and are very social and involved in parenting our children. So that's a very wholesome evolved trait, honestly. Humans are very lovey-dovey, so we don't have baculum. Anyway, this bone is one of my favorites because it was such a strange concept to me when I first found out about its existence.
